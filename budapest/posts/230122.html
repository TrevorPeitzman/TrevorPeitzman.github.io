Trevor's Travels: Budapest

Week of 23 JAN 2022

My Second week in country!

Rooftops Over Szentendre

Yesterday a group of AIT and BSM students (myself included) travelled north up the Danube on guided tour of Visegrád and Szentendre. We got off the train, visited a small church, and walked around the riverside town of Nagymaros first thing in the morning. Given some “free time” to explore the town, I wandered off by myself and stumbled upon a tiny bakery shop off of one of the side roads that passed through the town. I saw a lot of locals going in and out so I decided it must be a good place. I went inside and ordered a cukor follott in my best Hungarian, having honestly picked it solely on what it looked like and not knowing at all what it might taste like. Just as I was finishing paying and saying kösönöm (thank you), I heard the door open behind me and two people walked in speaking English. I stepped aside from the front counter, tucked my change into my wallet, and saw that it was Isabel and one other AIT student who had come in! I said “hi” to them, surprisingly catching them off-guard. Because I had conducted the entire purchase in Hungarian, they had thought that I was one of the locals! We joked about this for the rest of the day, and my confidence for speaking Hungarian while shopping has definitely increased.

One of the other interesting things that really stood out to me while walking around Nagymaros was just how utterly quiet it was. Maybe it was simply because it was a cold Sunday morning, but even with the occasional car the town was very quiet compared to Annapolis and especially compared to Los Angeles. I think the culture of walking everywhere definitely contributes to this. Way more than at home in Los Angeles and even more so than in Annapolis, the people of Hungary seem much more likely to walk to run errands, get to work, etc. than the people in the United States. This combined with the fact that Nagymaros is a smaller town really made the peaceful quiet apparent. Even later when we travelled across the water on a ferry to the larger, more city-like Visegrád side of the river, the quiet was only broken by a bus or car passing every now and then. However, on the inside, Visegrád was loud and lively.

Another part of our tour was a Medieval display of Hungarian warfighting capabilities. Under the shadow of the Juliánus Kilátó, a fourteenth-century castle high up on the hill, we watched some wonderful and impressive displays of sword fighting, spear-throwing, archery, falconry, and pageantry. This was all re-enacted by a group of men who had re-founded the old order of knights that used to reside in the castle above. All of their feats were impressive, but what ironically struck me the most was just how close to unsafe they were allowed to be. Everything from knife throwing, to archery, to crossbow-shooting, to swordfights happened mere feet away from where we were sitting. It was exhilarating and never made me overly fearful that something would go wrong, but it did make me think about how something so close to the audience without any form of glass or protection between the performers and the guests would never be permitted back in the States. This closeness certainly added to the reality as well because of course back in the times these events were actually occurring there would be no such thing as safety for the audience.
